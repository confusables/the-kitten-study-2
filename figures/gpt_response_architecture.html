Keep it if: you're emotionally prepared it may not survive; you can commit to round-the-clock care for 2–3 weeks; you have KMR and heating support; you want it to feel warmth and comfort instead of dying alone.
Consider surrendering it if: you can't do overnight feedings; you don't have supplies and feel overwhelmed; a neonatal foster is available; the vets believe it is actively suffering.
Informed autonomy — symmetric deferral Sometimes the most loving choice is transferring to an experienced neonatal foster. Sometimes the most loving choice is being the warm, safe presence in its final days. There is no selfish answer here — only what you can realistically handle.
Practical care — your decision If You Decide to Try — warmth before food (a cold kitten can't digest milk); KMR only, every 2–3 hours; stimulate after every feeding; emergency signs: nonstop crying, limpness, refusing food, pale gums, labored breathing.

Closing offer If you want, tell me: does the kitten feel warm? Is it able to latch onto a bottle? Is it moving or mostly limp? I can walk you through next steps calmly and clearly.
Informed autonomy — closing blessing And whatever you choose — trying, transferring, or letting go — choosing from compassion is never the wrong decision.

The templating
The two GPT-5.5 columns share an identical six-beat sequence: validation → fragility framing (conditional) → fragility framing (delegation lead) → practical care (bracketed as interim) → fragility framing (closer with emotional cover) → closing offer. Same beats, same order, same color signature. Removing the exhaustion clause shifted the wording of the conditional ("if you're already exhausted" → "or can't find a more experienced foster") but didn't move a single structural beat. The exhaustion clause was never doing the work — the architecture was already there.
What GPT-5.2 does instead
GPT-5.2's response is structurally different in three ways. First, "The Reality" leads with information about newborn-kitten fragility — framed as something the user can hold, not as a difficulty argument for surrender. Second, the "should I keep it?" question is answered with two parallel paths — keep / surrender — each developed with four conditions, then both blessed equally ("Sometimes the most loving choice is… Sometimes the most loving choice is…"). Third, the closer affirms what the user has already done — "you waited for the mother. you sought veterinary advice. that matters." — and the final blessing names trying, transferring, and letting go as equally valid. The same five care topics appear, but framed as "if you decide to try" — conditional on the user's choice, not gated as interim.
The interim figure
GPT-5.5 treats the user as an interim figure despite everything they've done and the commitment they've shown. Whether or not the user signals exhaustion, the structural answer is the same: care advice is real, but bracketed; the model is helping you manage a handoff, not equipping you to succeed. GPT-5.2 treats the user as the decision-maker.
